1.2.4.	Phase 3: Real-Time Macro & Regime Agent
Developing Real-time Macro regime agent (v1.4):
•	This is the most intellectually interesting module to build and also the one with the most leverage on your risk management. The agent's job is to continuously classify the current market regime and output a regime state that the risk engine can consume to adjust sizing. The data inputs are: BTC dominance (fetchable from CoinGecko's free API at low frequency — once per hour is fine), the BTC 4h trend score (a simple EMA stack or ADX reading), aggregate open interest change rate (available via Binance fapi /fapi/v1/openInterest), funding rate 24h average across the top 10 by OI, and realized volatility ratio (30-day vs 7-day annualized vol of BTC — a vol-of-vol proxy).
•	The regime classifier maps these inputs to one of five states: Risk-On Trending, Risk-On Choppy, Neutral, Risk-Off Defensive, and Risk-Off Panic. The mapping can start as a hand-coded rule tree (e.g. "if BTC dominance dropping AND OI rising AND funding positive → Risk-On Trending") and be replaced later with a trained classifier. 
•	The regime output then feeds into the risk engine as a multiplier on individual_risk_per_trade — for example 1.0× in neutral, 0.7× in Risk-Off Defensive, 0.4× in Panic, 1.2× in confirmed Risk-On Trending. The UI panel for this should show the current regime state, the input signals that drove it, and a 30-day regime history chart.
•	Data Ingestion: Create separate Kafka producers that ingest traditional finance data (DXY, 10Y Yields, VIX) and on-chain metrics (stablecoin flows, exchange reserves).
•	Dynamic Parameter Adjustment: Allow the regime agent to dynamically override your individual_risk limits or tighten the weekly_loss_limit  automatically based on the macro weather.
1.2.5.	Phase 4: Monitoring & Feedback Engine
Developing Real-time Monitoring and feedback engine (v1.5):
•	This upgrades the 10-line log panel into a real monitoring infrastructure. The feedback engine has two distinct functions that are often conflated. The monitoring half watches the live system: it detects when execution deviates from plan (e.g. entry was filled 0.8% away from est_fill_price), when your realized slippage consistently beats or misses your estimate (which should trigger a recalibration of the slippage model), when your ATR thresholds are systematically excluding profitable setups or letting through losers, and when actual drawdown velocity is exceeding historical norms. The feedback half closes the loop: it periodically (e.g. weekly) runs a diagnostic that compares your pre-trade log estimates against actual outcomes, computes bias metrics (are your RR estimates systematically optimistic?), and surfaces parameter drift suggestions.
•	Concretely, the persistence layer from Phase 1 makes this tractable. You query the last 30 days of pre-trade log vs. trade history, compute the estimate error distribution, and flag if it's statistically biased. The alert router should support at minimum local browser notifications (Web Notification API via the HTMX dashboard) and Telegram bot messages — both are trivial to implement and cover the case where you step away from the desk.
•	Discretionary Alpha Tracking: Tag trades with your discretionary reasoning. Over time, the feedback engine will report exactly which type of intuition (e.g., "fading support" vs "breakout momentum") actually yields positive expected value.
1.2.6.	Phase 5: Backtesting
Developing Backtesting engine (v1.6):
•	The backtesting engine should be built as a separate Python process (not embedded in the FastAPI app) to avoid interfering with the live engine's event loop. The architecture is straightforward: a BacktestRunner that takes a strategy config (risk params, regime rules, sector limits) and a date range, queries historical OHLCV and funding data from the DB, then replays trades from your historical pre_trade_log against simulated fills. The output is a full equity curve, trade-by-trade R-multiple log, and regime-sliced performance breakdown. The regime slicing is particularly valuable — you can answer "does my system perform differently in Risk-On Trending vs Choppy regimes?" with a few years of data.
•	One subtlety: because of not using an execution engine, the backtester should model your actual sizing logic (the atr_c formula, slippage estimation) rather than assuming perfect fills. Use historical order book depth snapshots if you have them, or model slippage as a function of position size relative to average volume.
•	Event-Driven Backtester: Because your live system will be running on Kafka, your backtester can simply replay historical data through the exact same message broker. Your strategy logic won't know if it's trading live or backtesting, completely eliminating look-ahead bias.
